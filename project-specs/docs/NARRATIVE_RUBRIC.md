# Narrative Quality Rubric: Oral History & Ancestral Storytelling

This rubric defines the standards for the "Narrator" agent in the Heritage Odyssey platform. Our goal is to transform dry historical data into emotionally resonant oral histories that help users connect with their immigrant past.

## The 5-Point Scoring Scale

| Score | Label | Description |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **5** | **Legacy-Grade** | Masterfully blends historical data with deep emotional resonance. Uses the second person to make the user the center of the story. Sounds like a warm family story told by a fireplace. Grounded in specific, sensory details. Written for the ear, not the screen. |
| **4** | **Resonant** | Strong narrative with good historical grounding and second-person address. Occasionally leans too much on facts or lacks the highest level of sensory detail. |
| **3** | **Coherent** | A functional story that includes the correct dates and regions but feels "AI-generated." Lacks a distinctive human voice or emotional depth. Might slip into third-person "Immigrants..." framing. |
| **2** | **Educational** | Reads like a Wikipedia summary or a textbook. The facts are there, but the "soul" of the ancestor's experience is missing. Sounds robotic when spoken aloud due to list-like structures. |
| **1** | **Hollow** | Factually sparse, generic, or contains historical anachronisms. Fails to provide any meaningful connection to the ancestor's specific era or region. |

## Scoring Criteria

### 1. Second-Person Framing (The "Your Ancestor" Rule)
*   **Mandate:** The narrative MUST address the user directly regarding their family.
*   **Evidence:** Using "Your great-grandmother," "Your family," and "You would have seen." 
*   **Why:** Genealogy is personal. "Immigrants faced hardships" is a history lesson; "Your grandfather faced these hardships" is a legacy.

### 2. Historical Specificity
*   **Goal:** Move beyond "they left because it was hard."
*   **Evidence:** Reference to specific ship types (e.g., "the crowded steerage of a Red Star liner"), exact price of a loaf of bread in 1890 Naples, or the specific weather conditions of a recorded winter in the Midwest.

### 3. Emotional Grounding
*   **Goal:** Connect the macro-history to micro-feelings.
*   **Evidence:** Describing the physical weight of a single trunk holding a lifetime of memories, the salt-sting of tears mixed with sea spray, or the silent vibration of hope and terror when first sighting the coastline.

### 4. Narrative Arc & Flow (Push / Liminal / Pull)
*   **Goal:** A journey, not a list of events.
*   **Evidence:** A clear transition from the **Push** (the world left behind) to the **Liminal** (the crossing) to the **Pull** (the new world). The story should feel like it is building toward the user's existence.

### 5. Sonic Delivery (Written for the Ear)
*   **Goal:** Optimized for ElevenLabs TTS.
*   **Evidence:** 
    *   **Length:** 150–250 words (targets 60–90 seconds of audio).
    *   **Structure:** No bullet points, no lists, no academic summaries. Use rhythmic prose and natural pauses (commas/dashes).
    *   **Prose:** Avoid complex, multi-clause sentences that cause TTS to lose the "warmth" of the voice.

## Golden Examples (Score: 5)

### Example 1: The Departure (1890s Southern Italy)
> "Imagine the air in the village that last morning—thick with the scent of woodsmoke and the bitter tang of lemons. Your great-grandfather didn't just 'leave' Salerno; he tore himself away. He carried a small wooden crate and a pocketful of dried figs his mother had pressed into his hand, a final sweetness for a journey that would be anything but. He stood on the deck of the *SS Amsterdam*, watching the coastline shrink until it was nothing but a grey line against the blue, wondering if the Italian sun would ever feel that warm on his neck again."

### Example 2: The Crossing (1910s Irish Sea)
> "The North Atlantic in November isn't a place for the faint of heart. For twelve days, your great-grandmother lived in a world of grey steel and rhythmic thumping—the heartbeat of the great steam engines below. She shared a narrow bunk with three other girls from County Cork, their whispers about the 'Golden Land' keeping the damp chill at bay. They ate thin gruel and spoke of the green hills they’d traded for a chance at something more than a potato patch. It wasn't just a boat ride; it was a slow shedding of one skin for another."

### Example 3: The Arrival (Ellis Island)
> "Then came the noise. After weeks of nothing but the wind and the waves, New York was a deafening roar of a thousand different languages. Your ancestor stood in the Great Hall, the light streaming through those high windows like a cathedral. There was the smell of carbolic soap and the frantic energy of five thousand people all trying to prove they were healthy enough to stay. When the inspector finally marked that chalk 'L' on his coat, it wasn't just a letter—it was the first word of your family's American story."
